Cybersecurity Note #4

Authentication vs Authorization

What is Authentication?

Authentication is the process of verifying the identity of a user, device, or system.

In simple terms:

Authentication answers the question:

“Who are you?”

Before a system grants access, it must first confirm that you are who you claim to be.

Examples of Authentication

* Logging into LinkedIn with your email and password
* Using a fingerprint to unlock a smartphone
* Entering a One-Time Password (OTP)

Common Authentication Methods

* Passwords
* PINs
* Fingerprints
* Facial recognition
* Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

What is Authorization?

Authorization determines what an authenticated user is allowed to do.

In simple terms:

Authorization answers the question:

“What are you allowed to access?”

Authorization happens after authentication.

Examples of Authorization

* A user can view their own LinkedIn profile
* An administrator can manage user accounts
* An employee can access company files relevant to their role

Authentication vs Authorization

Authentication verifies identity.

Authorization determines permissions.

Authentication

* Answers: “Who are you?”
* Happens first
* Example: Logging into LinkedIn

Authorization

* Answers: “What are you allowed to do?”
* Happens after authentication
* Example: Editing your own LinkedIn profile

Real-Life Example

Imagine entering an office building.

Authentication

The security guard checks your ID card to confirm who you are.

Authorization

Once inside, your role determines which rooms you can access.

* A visitor may enter the reception area.
* An employee may access workspaces.
* A manager may access restricted offices.

Why It Matters

Authentication and authorization help protect systems and data from unauthorized access.

Without authentication, anyone could claim to be someone else.

Without authorization, users could access information they should not be allowed to see.

Both are essential components of cybersecurity.

Key Takeaway

Authentication verifies identity.

Authorization determines permissions.

A secure system needs both to ensure that the right people have the right level of access.
